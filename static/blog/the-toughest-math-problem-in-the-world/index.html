Worked Examples

Example 1

Problem: If a farmer has 12 apples and he gives away 3 apples to each of his 4 friends, how many apples does he have left?
Step 1: Start by calculating the total number of apples the farmer gives away. He gives 3 apples to each of 4 friends, so multiply 3 by 4 to get 12.
Step 2: Subtract the number of apples given away from the initial total. The farmer starts with 12 apples and gives away 12, leaving him with 12 - 12.
Step 3: The farmer has 0 apples left after giving away the apples to his friends.

Example 2

Problem: A train travels from City A to City B at a speed of 60 miles per hour. If the distance between the two cities is 180 miles, and the train stops for 30 minutes during the journey, how long in hours will it take the train to reach City B?
Step 1: Calculate the travel time without stops by dividing the distance by the speed. 180 miles / 60 miles per hour equals 3 hours.
Step 2: Convert the stopping time into hours. 30 minutes is equal to 0.5 hours.
Step 3: Add the stopping time to the travel time. 3 hours + 0.5 hours equals 3.5 hours.
Step 4: The train will take a total of 3.5 hours to reach City B.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

One common mistake students make when tackling the toughest math problem in the world is misinterpreting the problem statement. This can lead to incorrect assumptions and errors in calculations. To avoid this, always take the time to thoroughly read and understand the problem before attempting a solution.

Another frequent error is neglecting to verify the final solution. Students often stop once they find an answer, without double-checking if it meets all the problem’s requirements. Always verify your solution by revisiting the problem statement and cross-referencing your calculations to ensure accuracy.
