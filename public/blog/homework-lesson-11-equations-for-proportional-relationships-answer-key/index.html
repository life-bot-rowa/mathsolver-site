Step-by-Step: How to Solve Homework Lesson 11 Equations For Proportional Relationships Answer Key

1

Step 1: Identify the Variables

The first step in solving equations for proportional relationships is to identify the two variables involved. These variables represent the quantities that are changing in relation to each other. For example, in a problem involving distance and fuel consumption, the variables might be miles traveled and gallons of gas used. Identifying these variables helps set the stage for finding the constant of proportionality.

2

Step 2: Determine the Constant of Proportionality

Once you have identified the variables, the next step is to determine the constant of proportionality, k. This constant represents the rate at which one variable changes relative to the other. To find k, you can use the formula k = y/x, where y is the dependent variable and x is the independent variable. Calculating k gives you the key to setting up the proportional equation.

3

Step 3: Write the Proportional Equation

With the constant of proportionality known, you can now write the equation for the proportional relationship. This equation takes the form y = kx. Writing the equation allows you to express the relationship between the variables in a mathematical format, which can then be used to solve for unknown values.

4

Step 4: Solve for the Unknown

The final step is to use the proportional equation to solve for the unknown variable. Substitute the known values into the equation, and solve for the unknown. This process involves basic algebraic manipulation, such as multiplying or dividing both sides of the equation to isolate the unknown variable. Once solved, you will have the solution to the problem.

Worked Examples

Example 1

Problem: A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour to make 12 cookies. How many cups of flour are needed to make 30 cookies?
Step 1: Identify the variables: Flour (F) and Cookies (C).
Step 2: Determine the constant of proportionality: k = F/C = 2/12 = 1/6.
Step 3: Write the proportional equation: F = (1/6)C.
Step 4: Solve for the unknown (F) when C = 30: F = (1/6) * 30 = 5 cups. Therefore, 5 cups of flour are needed to make 30 cookies.

Example 2

Problem: A car travels 180 miles using 6 gallons of gas. If the car continues to maintain this rate, how many gallons of gas will it need to travel 450 miles?
Step 1: Identify the variables: Miles (M) and Gallons (G).
Step 2: Determine the constant of proportionality: k = G/M = 6/180 = 1/30.
Step 3: Write the proportional equation: G = (1/30)M.
Step 4: Solve for the unknown (G) when M = 450: G = (1/30) * 450 = 15 gallons. Therefore, 15 gallons of gas are needed to travel 450 miles.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

One common mistake students make when working on homework lesson 11 equations for proportional relationships answer key is confusing the dependent and independent variables. It’s crucial to correctly identify which variable depends on the other to set up the equation accurately. Another frequent error is incorrectly calculating the constant of proportionality. Students should double-check their division to ensure that k is computed correctly.

Additionally, students sometimes forget to maintain the units of measurement consistent throughout the problem. Mixing units can lead to incorrect answers, so it's important to keep track of them from start to finish. By being mindful of these common pitfalls, students can improve their accuracy and confidence in solving proportional relationship problems.
